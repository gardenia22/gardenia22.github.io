Industry Analysis

The AI Money Flow

How capital moves through the AI economy — from advertisers and enterprises to chips, data centers, and power grids.

Overview

Two Overlapping Money Flows

A useful way to read the AI industry is as two overlapping capital flows. The first is operating cash flow: end users, enterprises, developers, and advertisers pay for products. The second is the capital-expenditure flow: hyperscalers, AI labs, neoclouds, and investors commit huge sums to GPUs, custom chips, data centers, networking, cooling, land, and power — before the final AI service is necessarily profitable.

Capital Loops

How Each Company's Loop Works

The most important structural feature of today's AI economy is circular capital flows: companies invest in each other, then commit to buying each other's services. These loops are not automatically bad — they can coordinate long-term supply — but they blur the line between independent end-user demand and strategic financing.

Infrastructure Deals

Major Public Commitments

While exact GPU prices and chip invoices are rarely public, major strategic deals reveal the shape of spending at each layer.

Compute & Cloud

Meta + CoreWeave: $21B cloud capacity expansion through Dec 2032, on top of an earlier $14.2B deal — dedicated AI capacity including Nvidia Vera Rubin.
OpenAI + Oracle: ~$300B of compute over ~5 years.
OpenAI + Azure: $250B of services committed. Microsoft remains primary cloud partner through 2032.
Anthropic + AWS: $100B+ over 10 years; up to 5 GW of Trainium capacity.

Custom Silicon

Meta + Broadcom: custom AI chip deal through 2029, initial commitment of 1+ GW of computing capacity.
OpenAI + Broadcom: co-develop custom AI accelerators; 10 GW planned deployment, rollout starting H2 2026.
Google + Broadcom: custom AI chips and rack components through 2031. Broadcom also supplying Anthropic ~3.5 GW via Google AI processors from 2027.

Power

Microsoft + Constellation: 20-year PPA, ~835 MW from Three Mile Island Unit 1 restart.
Amazon + Talen: 1,920 MW nuclear through 2042 for AWS data centers.
Meta: nuclear agreements totaling up to 6.6 GW by 2035 (Vistra, TerraPower, Oklo). Meta pays full cost of data-center energy.

Power & Energy

How Data Centers Pay Energy Companies

Power is becoming a binding constraint. Data-center operators secure energy through utility service, power purchase agreements, direct energy contracts, colocation, renewable/nuclear matching, and sometimes behind-the-meter generation.

Reuters Breakingviews estimated a 100 MW AI data center can cost more than $4 billion — with ~70% going to servers and GPUs and the rest to buildings, electrical systems, networking, security, and cooling. Grid connections and gas-turbine supply can create multi-year bottlenecks.

Core Question

The Fundamental AI Profit Equation

The most important economic question is not "who has the best model?" It is whether AI revenue per user, query, or workflow can exceed the full cost of delivering it.

For Google and Meta, AI can be profitable even if users never pay directly — because better AI increases ad relevance, engagement, conversion, and automation. For Microsoft and Amazon, AI is profitable if it increases cloud consumption, enterprise software pricing, and platform lock-in. For OpenAI and Anthropic, profitability depends on whether subscription, API, enterprise, and ad revenue can exceed enormous compute commitments.

How to Read the AI Economy

01 End-user and advertiser cash enters at the application layer. Enterprise cash enters at the cloud/API/software layer. Investor and balance-sheet capital enters at the infrastructure layer.
02 Most of that capital flows upstream to chips, data centers, power, and suppliers — before AI applications are necessarily profitable.
03 The winners are companies that already own profitable distribution, control scarce infrastructure, or turn models into products with durable pricing power.
04 The circularity is real. Microsoft funds and supplies OpenAI. Amazon invests in Anthropic. Google builds TPUs and defends Search. Meta uses ad profits to buy compute and build its own models. Nvidia sells into nearly everyone.
05 The open question: can AI-native applications grow into profits large enough to justify the infrastructure commitments now being made?
